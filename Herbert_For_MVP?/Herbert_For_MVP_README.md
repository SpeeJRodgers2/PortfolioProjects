The other driver of the MVP race is the team record because people use wins as a QB stat. Here’s Maye’s stats compared to the averages of the last 5 MVP winners including Matthew Stafford for the 2025 season.

If you’re still reading, please follow me on this path of nuance and context as to why Sam had every right to vote for Justin Herbert. Especially if you think Drake Maye is the rightful winner. I’ll be comparing them throughout the rest of this paper, and you might be surprised at the similarities between them just as much as the differences this season.

The first piece of nuance that I believe should be taken into consideration about why Drake Maye shouldn’t get the MVP is that his stats and the team’s win total are buoyed by their incredibly easy schedule. The combined record of their opponents was 113-176 which comes out to a .391 win percentage. This is tied for the 3rd lowest win percentage in the last 50 seasons! A HISTORICALLY easy schedule. They played 11 games against teams that aren’t returning their head coach, tied for THE MOST in NFL history, tied with the 1925 Frankford Yellow Jackets… Comparably the chargers had 6 games against teams not returning their head coach and their opponents combined record was 135-153 which comes out to a .469 win percentage. It’s not a hard schedule by any means, but it’s still a significant difference because of how historically easy the Patriots' schedule ended up being. This difference shouldn’t be discounted when comparing the season that Maye and Herbert had. 

The second piece of nuance is looking at the talent around them. If you compare the rushing attacks of each team, the Patriots were better in every way.

![Pats vs. Chargers Main RB Efficiency Comp](https://github.com/SpeeJRodgers2/PortfolioProjects/blob/main/Herbert_For_MVP%3F/Charts_%26_Graphs/Team_Main_RB_Efficiency.png)

This chart looks at only efficiency metrics, so they can be directly compared. The chart is titled “Main RBs” because the efficiency metrics being compared are from the top two running-backs of each team. Rhamondre Stevenson & TreVeyon Henderson for the Patriots and Omarion Hampton & Kimani Vidal for the Chargers. I did this so the data isn’t skewed by RBs that only got a few carries throughout the season for either team, like Terrell Jennings or Hassan Haskins. The data doesn’t end up being very different regardless. A more standard comparison of these backfield duos has the two Patriots RBs having 326 more yards and 9 more TDs on just 31 more carries. A common theme throughout this paper will be the mentioning of the Chargers injury woes. This played out in Omarion Hamptons season where he started just 4 games and played in 9 because of a fractured ankle in the Chargers’ week 5 game that forced him to IR. He missed 7 games from this injury. Patriots RB Rhamondre Stevenson missed 3 games with a toe injury as well. However, 2nd round pick TreVeyon Henderson certainly picked up the slack a lot more than 6th rounder Kimani Vidal did for the Chargers. It’s common ball knowledge that a good running game can elevate QB play, but this is especially true in today’s NFL because of all the 2 high shell coverage that’s taken over as the predominant defensive scheme. It’s a huge advantage to be able to get a defense out of these two safety looks to make things easier on the QB. All that to say that Herbert had a little more on his shoulders because of this.

As far as the help from the receiving core (WRs & TEs) around each QB, Maye arguably had a better supporting cast here as-well. Looking at pass catchers with 30 or more receptions for each team, which ends up being the top 5 pass catchers for each team for 10 total players, the Patriots have 5 of the top 7 in catch rate, 3 of the top 4, and the top 2 in Stefon Diggs & Kayshon Boutte.

![Pats vs. Chargers Top 10 Pass Catchers Catch Rate](https://github.com/SpeeJRodgers2/PortfolioProjects/blob/main/Herbert_For_MVP%3F/Charts_%26_Graphs/Patriots_vs_Chargers_Top_10_Pass_Catchers_CR_%25.png)

The most surprising part of this is that the bottom 3 players in catch rate out of this group are all Chargers and they aren’t who you’d expect! In order it’s Keenan Allen, Quentin Johnston, and last out of the group is Ladd McConkey. Catching the ball is the easiest way to help out your QB. This is more succinctly captured in each QB’s drop rate where Herbert’s is 6.1% and Maye’s is 4.1%. This difference doesn’t sound like much, but it comes out to 31 drops for Herbert vs. 20 drops for Maye. You could argue that “Herbert wasn’t as accurate, look at the difference in their completion percentages.” Herbert had a 66.4% completion percentage compared to Maye’s league leading 72.0%. This argument would be a valid one if we didn’t have the data on accurate throw rate and catchable throw rate. Herbert actually had a higher accurate throw percentage than Maye albeit slightly, 61.5% vs. 61.0%, and Herbert’s catchable throw percent was slightly lower than Maye’s, 77.3% vs. 79.3%. The fact that Herbert was this accurate of a passer will be even more impressive after the next piece of context, the offensive line.

I can understand if you write off the differences in each QB’s rushing and receiving casts as marginal. However, the difference in their offensive line play cannot be overstated! I’m aware that everyone thinks the Patriots offensive line is one of the worst units in the league after the Superbowl, but to be fair, a lot of teams' O-lines looked like that against the historically good defensive unit of the Seattle Seahawks. If you watched a Chargers game this year, you likely saw a similar performance from whatever defensive unit they were up against at that time. Here are some amazing graphics from Ben Baldwin, @benbbaldwin on X, showing offensive line grades that lay out the massive difference in what each QB was working with.

The Patriots offensive line grades are in another stratosphere compared to the Chargers. Two different sources have the chargers with a 0 grade on the season… It literally can’t get worse than that! Here is one more graphic from Ben to hammer home the point. 

![2025 Run & Pass block Grades](https://github.com/SpeeJRodgers2/PortfolioProjects/blob/main/Herbert_For_MVP%3F/Charts_%26_Graphs/Run_and_Pass_Block_Grades_2025.png)

The Chargers are by themselves! The context behind this is all about injuries. Former second team all-pro tackle Rashawn Slater tore his patellar tendon before the season even started, forcing him to miss the entire season, and Pro Football Writers of America and ESPN all rookie team tackle Joe Alt played only 6 games. He suffered a high ankle sprain in week 4 before returning in week 8 and suffering a season ending high ankle fracture in week 9. I put the accolades of these players up because it emphasizes the difference in level of play that was likely coming in behind them after their injuries. Their back up tackles didn’t stay healthy either with Bobby Hart and Austin Deculus sustaining injuries as well. This forced them to make a move for Trevor Penning who is a former first rounder, but they acquired him by trading a 6th round pick. That should tell you how well he’s been playing. All of this led to a total of 32 different offensive line combinations throughout the season, the most in the NFL, that Justin Herbert had to deal with. For a quick comparison, the Browns had the 2nd most offensive line combinations and the Saints had the 3rd most with them coming in at 30 and 29 respectively. They went 5-12 and 6-11 while the Chargers went 11-6 in large part due to Herbert elevating this team. The better defense argument is mute here because the Browns and Saints both had good defenses sitting in the top 10 in yards allowed per game.

The Patriots did suffer some offensive line injuries late in the season, specifically week 12 when their two rookies Jared Wilson and Will Campbell got hurt in the same game. Even with these injuries, the Patriots only had a total of 5 different offensive line combinations through all of the 2025 season. It’s also key to point out that the drop off in play from two rookie offensive lineman isn’t near what the Chargers had to deal with when they lost two of the best tackles in the NFL.

Knowing all this about the massive difference in offensive line play leads to some very pertinent questions like, “How are Drake Maye’s and Justin Herbert’s pressure and sack stats so similar?” Herbert was pressured 267 times which is the most of any QB in 2025 and any QB in the entire 5 year sample size of data. He was sacked 54 times which is 2nd behind Geno Smith and Cam Ward at 55 in the 2025 season. Maye was right there with 266 pressures, 2nd in 2025, and sacked 47 times, good for the 3rd most in 2025. 

![Herbert vs. Maye 2025 Sacks/Pressure Stats](https://github.com/SpeeJRodgers2/PortfolioProjects/blob/main/Herbert_For_MVP%3F/Charts_%26_Graphs/Herbert_vs_Maye_Sacks_Pressures_2025.png)

The answer to why these stats are so similar while the offensive line play was anything but, is simple. The sacks and pressures are mostly Drake Maye’s fault, and that is bad QB play. I got percentiles for every critical statistic that is relevant to proving this point to really hammer it home. If you don’t know, a percentile will give a percentage that indicates the position of a player's stat relative to the rest of every other player in the dataset. For example, Drake Maye’s average time to throw stat in 2025, which measures the average time from the snap to the throw of a pass, was in the 90th percentile. This means that his time to throw was greater than or equal to 90% of every other player in the dataset, so he held the ball longer on average than 90% of every other QB in the last 5 seasons. Comparatively, Herbert has 55th percentile time to throw which means he’s right around the average QB in the last 5 seasons. I’m using percentiles because the difference in their average time to throw is very small, just 0.22 seconds, but this small difference in time is actually incredibly significant when it comes to QB play in the NFL. The difference between the lowest and highest average time to throw in the last 5 seasons is only 0.88 seconds, not even a full second! 

![Herbert vs. Maye 2025 TTT/TTP Stats](https://github.com/SpeeJRodgers2/PortfolioProjects/blob/main/Herbert_For_MVP%3F/Charts_%26_Graphs/Herbert_vs_Maye_TTT_TTP_Comp_2025.png)

Maye’s average time to pressure was also 90th percentile while Herbert’s was just 30th. This also emphasizes the difference in offensive line play from both teams. Again, the difference is only 0.24 seconds, but the percentiles show just how big of a difference that really is. We also have data on how many sacks are the QB’s fault. Maye is charted with 23 of his 47 sacks as being on him while Herbert only has 12 out of his 54, almost half of Maye’s sacks are on him vs. just 22% for Herbert. Maybe Herbert is checking the ball down or throwing it away more than Maye, and that’s why their sack and pressure numbers are so close? Nope, Herbert checks it down at a lower rate, 7.0% vs. 9.3%, and they both have 20 throw aways on the season. All this paints the picture that everyone can see when they watch a Patriots game. While Maye is behind one of the best graded offensive lines in the NFL, he is allowing pressures and sacks to happen. Now, just imagine if he had the offensive line play that Herbert had this year! He might’ve broken sack and pressure records because he holds the ball and doesn’t handle pressure well. This played out throughout the playoffs. You can argue that’s because of the level of defense he went up against, but that excuse isn’t valid for the regular season where he had one of the easiest schedules in NFL history as shown earlier.
